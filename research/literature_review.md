# Literature Review: Market Regime Discovery

## 1. What is a Market Regime?
A "market regime" refers to a persistent underlying environment or state in which financial markets operate. Markets are notoriously non-stationary; the statistical properties (mean returns, volatility, correlation between assets) do not remain constant over time. Instead, they shift discretely between different phases. 

Commonly discussed regimes include:
* **Bull Markets:** Steady, positive returns with low volatility.
* **Bear Markets:** Negative returns, often accompanied by elevated volatility.
* **High-Volatility / Crisis:** Sharp drawdowns, massive intraday swings, and high cross-asset correlation.
* **Sideways / Range-bound:** Low returns, mean-reverting behavior.

## 2. Academic Literature & Core Models

### 2.1 The Failure of Stationarity
Traditional financial models (like the Black-Scholes options pricing model or Modern Portfolio Theory) often assume that asset returns are normally distributed and stationary. However, academic research has consistently shown that financial returns exhibit "fat tails" (extreme events happen more often than a normal distribution predicts) and volatility clustering (Mandelbrot, 1963). Regime discovery attempts to explain these anomalies by suggesting that the market jumps between different normal distributions.

### 2.2 Hamilton's Regime-Switching Model (1989)
The foundational paper for modern regime discovery is James D. Hamilton's 1989 paper, *"A New Approach to the Economic Analysis of Nonstationary Time Series and the Business Cycle."* 
Hamilton proposed that the economy shifts between fast-growth and recession states. He introduced **Markov-Switching Models**, where the transition between states follows a Markov process (the probability of tomorrow's state depends only on today's state). This became the basis for using **Hidden Markov Models (HMMs)** in finance.

### 2.3 Clustering Approaches
Before HMMs became computationally accessible, many practitioners relied on simple clustering (like K-Means) or Gaussian Mixture Models (GMMs). While GMMs successfully group data into distinct distributions (e.g., a high-variance normal distribution and a low-variance normal distribution), they fail to account for the *temporal persistence* of markets. 

### 2.4 Deep Learning & Modern Approaches
Recent literature focuses on Deep Learning approaches. Autoencoders are used to compress raw price sequences into a "latent space" (a compressed mathematical representation). If the latent space separates into distinct clusters over time, those clusters represent regimes. Time-Series Transformers are also being actively researched to capture long-term dependencies that simple Markov models miss.

## 3. How Quantitative Funds Use Regime Modeling

Quantitative hedge funds and asset managers rarely trade a single strategy in all conditions. They use regime models for:

1. **Dynamic Asset Allocation:** Shifting portfolio weights. For example, moving from equities to bonds when the probability of a "Crisis Regime" exceeds a certain threshold.
2. **Strategy Selection:** A trend-following (momentum) strategy works exceptionally well in a Bull regime but bleeds money in a Sideways/Range-bound regime. Regime models act as "on/off" switches for underlying algorithmic strategies.
3. **Risk Management:** Volatility is not constant. By predicting a shift into a high-volatility regime, risk managers can automatically reduce leverage across the entire firm before the crisis fully materializes.

## Summary
The consensus in both academia and industry is that market returns are generated by hidden states. Effectively discovering these states allows for more accurate risk modeling and dynamic strategy allocation than assuming a single, continuous market environment.
